## Why Existing Homes Are the Real Opportunity

New construction gets most of the attention in the green building conversation, but the math tells a different story. The vast majority of homes that will be standing in 2050 have already been built. In the United States, more than 80 percent of the housing stock was constructed before modern energy codes existed. Transforming these existing buildings is not just an opportunity but a necessity for meeting climate goals.

Deep energy retrofits, comprehensive upgrades that reduce a home's energy consumption by 50 to 80 percent, are the most impactful intervention available to existing homeowners. They are also, when executed properly, among the best long-term investments a homeowner can make.

## What Makes a Retrofit "Deep"

A deep energy retrofit goes beyond replacing a furnace or adding attic insulation. It is a systematic approach that addresses the building as an integrated system. Core components typically include:

### Building Envelope Transformation

The envelope, meaning the walls, roof, foundation, windows, and doors that separate conditioned space from the outdoors, is the foundation of any deep retrofit:

- **Exterior insulation wraps** that eliminate thermal bridges and bring walls to R-25 or higher
- **Attic insulation upgrades** to R-60 or above with careful air sealing at the ceiling plane
- **Foundation insulation** addressing basement walls, crawlspace walls, or under-slab areas
- **High-performance window replacement** with triple-pane units appropriately specified for climate and orientation
- **Comprehensive air sealing** targeting a reduction to 2 ACH50 or below

### Mechanical System Right-Sizing

With a dramatically improved envelope, heating and cooling loads shrink substantially. This allows for:

- **Downsized heat pump systems** that cost less to install and operate
- **Elimination of ductwork** in many cases, using ductless mini-splits that avoid duct losses
- **Heat recovery ventilation** that provides fresh air without energy penalty
- **Heat pump water heaters** that replace gas or electric resistance units

### Electrification

Deep retrofits increasingly include full electrification, removing all combustion appliances and enabling the home to run entirely on grid electricity and on-site renewables.

## The Cost-Effectiveness Case

### Upfront Investment

A comprehensive deep energy retrofit for a typical single-family home ranges from $30,000 to $80,000 depending on size, condition, and climate zone. This is a significant investment, but it needs to be evaluated in the proper context.

### Energy Savings

Homes that undergo deep retrofits typically see energy cost reductions of $2,000 to $5,000 per year. In cold climates with high heating costs or warm climates with heavy cooling loads, savings can be even greater.

### Incentive Offsets

Federal tax credits, state rebates, and utility incentives can offset 25 to 40 percent of project costs. When these are factored in, the net investment drops considerably.

### Property Value Increase

Research consistently shows that energy-efficient homes sell for 3 to 8 percent more than comparable inefficient homes. For a $400,000 home, that represents $12,000 to $32,000 in additional equity.

### Comfort and Health Value

Perhaps the most underappreciated benefit is the dramatic improvement in comfort and indoor air quality. Homeowners who complete deep retrofits consistently report:

- Elimination of cold drafts and hot spots
- Consistent temperatures throughout the home
- Dramatically quieter interiors
- Better indoor air quality through controlled ventilation
- Reduced dust and allergens

These benefits are difficult to quantify financially but represent genuine improvements in quality of life.

## When to Pursue a Deep Retrofit

Deep retrofits are most cost-effective when aligned with other planned work. Key trigger points include:

- **Roof replacement** provides access for attic air sealing and insulation
- **Siding replacement** is the natural time for exterior insulation
- **HVAC end of life** is the opportunity to right-size and electrify
- **Kitchen or bathroom renovation** opens walls for insulation and air sealing
- **Foundation waterproofing** projects provide access for basement insulation

By integrating energy upgrades into planned maintenance and renovation, the incremental cost of deep performance improvements is much lower than standalone energy projects.

## Finding the Right Team

Deep energy retrofits require a different skill set than conventional renovation. The best results come from teams that include:

- A building science consultant or energy auditor who develops the overall strategy
- Insulation and air sealing contractors with verified experience in high-performance work
- HVAC professionals who understand heat pump sizing for tight envelopes
- A general contractor who can coordinate the sequencing of trades

## The Most Impactful Choice You Can Make

For existing homeowners who want to reduce their environmental impact and their energy bills simultaneously, deep energy retrofits offer the clearest path. They are not simple projects, but they are proven, practical, and increasingly well-supported by incentives and professional expertise. Every home that undergoes a deep retrofit is one more step toward a housing stock that is fit for the future.
